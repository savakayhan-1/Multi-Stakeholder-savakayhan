Abstract

Research overview

Independent cultural institutions play a critical role in artistic production, cultural participation, and social cohesion; however, following decades of relative stability, they now face profound pressures for transformation with regard to both funding and audience demand (Bakhshi & Throsby, 2010). Constraints on public budgets and increasing demands for accountability directly affect both the volume and conditions of funding, while audiences increasingly expect more customer-oriented strategies.

This study examines the multi-stakeholder engagement strategies of these institutions, encompassing funders, artists, and audiences (Freeman, 1984), from a comparative perspective. Denmark serves as the primary case study, while Sweden and the Netherlands provide comparative contextual models. A qualitative research design is adopted, combining literature review, sector report analysis, and digital content analysis of six institutions (January–March 2026).

The findings indicate that traditional marketing approaches are insufficient on their own; institutional success is increasingly associated with stakeholder-oriented communication strategies (Preece & Kerrigan, 2015). Most significantly, the concurrent analysis of real-time digital content with comprehensive sector reports, including VibeLab's (2025) Creative Footprint Copenhagen study (108 venues), the KUBI participatory process (314 stakeholder inputs, Kultur- og Fritidsforvaltningen, 2024), the Fair Practice Kultur research (100+ artists and institutions, Dansk Komponistforening & UKK, 2024), and Copenhagen's Segmenteringsundersøgelsen (5,623 respondents), empirically demonstrates that the funding model directly shapes not only institutional strategy but also digital communication capacity, and that no universal multi-stakeholder engagement framework can be adequate for independent cultural institutions across different national contexts.

Country contexts

Three models compared

Each country produces a distinct set of strategic requirements for independent cultural institutions.

Funding system

Denmark has one of Europe's strongest cultural funding systems. Total public expenditure on culture reached 27.3 billion DKK in 2023 (Danmarks Statistik, 2023), split roughly 58% central state / 42% municipalities. Statens Kunstfond (founded 1964) distributed over 692 million DKK in 2024 across 60+ programmes via 16 specialist committees. The Performing Arts Project Support Committee alone distributed 129.7 million DKK to small theatres and independent performing arts in 2024.

At municipal level, approximately 460 cultural houses (kulturhus) serve 16 million visitors annually with 2,600 staff and 8,900 volunteers (Kulturhusene i Danmark, 2023). A shift toward hybrid funding models, incorporating private companies, foundations and urban development projects, is increasingly observable.

Copenhagen scene & digital profile

The kulturhus concept in Copenhagen is built on functional, participatory identity rather than physical architecture. UNION Kulturhuset in Nørrebro hosts 400+ events annually under 20+ associations. Kulturhuset Islands Brygge hosts approximately 350 events per year. Both operate with hybrid funding and combine professional and amateur programming.

Denmark is among the world's highest digital participation countries: 99% internet penetration, 79.7% social media usage as of January 2024. Facebook leads with 78%+ market share; Instagram has ~2.5 million users. Among 15–24 year-olds, Snapchat, Instagram and TikTok dominate (DataReportal, 2024).

KUBI Plan 2025-2028: the funder's own dilemma
Copenhagen's Kulturhus- og Biblioteksplan 2025-2028 (KUBI) frames resources as "stones, people, materials", proposing actor-driven pilot models from 2026–2028 where selected cultural houses are operated by independent actors, associations or cooperatives. Yet the höringsnotat (formal public consultation process, 32 institutions, 2025) revealed sharp criticism: Dansk Teater warned that resources (midler) are invisible in the plan; Kulturens Selvstændige noted that cultural houses' activities are absent from national statistics entirely, making the sector structurally invisible to funders. The measurement paradox is not theoretical: it is built into the policy architecture.
Digital content finding: UNION Kulturhuset
5,499 Instagram followers vs. 11,809 Facebook likes, reflecting the national demographic split. Event announcements dominate both platforms; two-way dialogue formats are minimal. Walmsley's dialogue potential is present in theory; structural resource constraints prevent it in practice. The key question this raises: is the barrier to digital dialogue a lack of intent, or a lack of structural capacity? Denmark's case points to the latter.
Segmenteringsundersøgelsen 2023: audience data
5,623-person representative survey: 51% attend cultural events at least monthly; 74% wish they had more time for leisure interests. Cultural house attendance: 34%, yet only 26% feel they have sufficient knowledge of what cultural houses offer. Behaviour patterns, not demographics, are the strongest predictor of participation. Three key motivators: social dimension, flexible access, and peer/social media inspiration (Murray, 2005 confirmed empirically). The gap between participation and awareness is the central communication challenge.

The arm's-length paradox

Sweden institutionalised cultural policy relatively late: an independent cultural policy field emerged only from 1974; an independent Ministry of Culture was established in 1991 (Larsson & Svenson, 2001). The 1974 Riksdag resolution defined cultural participation as a universal welfare right. Funding is split roughly: state 47%, municipalities 45%, regional bodies 8%.

Sweden operates an arm's-length principle: government sets cultural policy objectives but delegates funding decisions to independent expert bodies, keeping political interference out of artistic choices. However, this produces a structural paradox for independent institutions: less than 5% of total state theatre subsidy reaches independent groups (Larsson & Svenson, 2001, p. 90). The 2024 national budget deepened this through significant cuts, including reductions in adult folk education, further compressing the independent sector (Harding, 2025). Rising rents and urban development pressure compound this further.

Sweden's artist protection mechanism that Denmark currently lacks: the MU-avtalet (2009), a binding exhibition and participation fee agreement for state institutions. The Musicians' Artists' Association (KLYS) fee recommendations serve as reference tariffs across all disciplines, a de facto partial partial Fair Practice standard (Konstnärernas Riksorganisation, 2024).

Fasching: the countermodel

Stockholm's infrastructure includes 89 museums, 130 libraries, 80 cultural centres and 93 theatre stages. Among independent institutions, Södra Teatern (est. 1859) hosts 500+ events and 250,000 visitors annually across 7 stages. Fasching (est. 1977) is Sweden's leading independent jazz venue.

Fasching's ownership structure is exceptional: majority owner is Föreningen Sveriges Jazzmusiker (FSJ, the musician association), minority partner is Faschings Vänner, an audience membership association of 4,000 members. Members pay 250 SEK/year for 25% concert discounts. This structure balances artist autonomy, audience ownership and funder accountability within a single institution.

Digital content finding: Södra Teatern vs. Fasching
Södra Teatern's website foregrounds venue hire and corporate events alongside cultural programming, with Coca-Cola, Heineken and Uber among featured partners. This signals an implicit shift from independent cultural venue toward commercial event space. Fasching, by contrast, maintains strong programmatic identity on Facebook (46,722 likes, high engagement), reflecting its musician-owned structure. The arm's-length paradox materialises in digital communication: constrained resources force independent institutions into commercial partnerships that dilute artistic identity.

Mature hybrid funding

Dutch cultural policy blends architect and guardian models, with arts funded at arm's length. A four-yearly 'cultuurnota' resets funding procedures and recipients (Loots et al., 2024). 71% of public cultural funding comes from municipalities (60%) and provincial authorities (11%). Private foundations play a significant role: Prins Bernard Cultuurfonds and VSBfonds support both large projects and artist grants.

The Netherlands' most distinctive structural feature: central government provides only ~30% of total cultural funding; the remaining 70% comes from municipalities, foundations, private sector and own revenues (Beroepkunstenaar.nl, 2025). This diversification is not merely policy preference; it is the structural enabler of institutional resilience. The six national cultural funds (Performing Arts Fund NL, Mondriaan Fund, Creative Industries Fund NL, and others) all require Fair Practice Code, Governance Code Cultuur and Diversity & Inclusion Code compliance as mandatory subsidy conditions for 2025-2028. Cultuurmonitor (2025) notes that even with this system, 32% of Dutch people found cultural events too expensive in 2024, confirming that hybrid funding reduces but does not eliminate access barriers.

Match-funding is increasingly central: every unit of private funding triggers a corresponding unit of public funding. Amsterdam's independent institutions, largely built on the legacy of 1980s squat movements, are organised as stichting (foundation) or vereniging (association) structures with ANBI (Algemeen Nut Beogende Instelling) status — a Dutch tax designation for public benefit organisations that allows donors to deduct gifts from income tax, making cultural donations structurally more attractive.

Melkweg & De Balie

Melkweg (est. 1970): ANBI foundation, all revenue reinvested in artist support. Main Instagram: 108,000 followers; plus @melkwegelectronic (12k), @melkweghiphop (15k), @melkwegfilm (1.6k), @melkwegexpo (9k). Instagram bio: "Old milk factory, now concerts, club, film, exhibitions and a cultural venue for young creators." Fair Practice Code and Diversiteit & Inclusie codes displayed transparently on website.

De Balie (est. 1982): 400+ events/year; Facebook: 58,000 likes, 70,000+ active engagement. Programme content live-streamed via Studio De Balie. This hybrid physical-digital model demonstrates Walmsley's (2016) co-creator thesis in practice: audiences become active participants in meaning-making.

Digital content finding: the most mature model
Melkweg's multi-channel segmentation is the most sophisticated digital strategy observed across the three countries. ANBI transparency on the website directly addresses funder accountability demands. Each Instagram channel speaks to a distinct artist-audience community. De Balie's live-streaming model converts digital access into genuine co-creation. The Netherlands' hybrid funding maturity directly enables this digital capacity, confirming the study's core finding: funding model shapes communication strategy.

The three countries diverge meaningfully on funding structure. Denmark's Statens Kunstfond-led system offers predictability and transparency for independent institutions. Sweden's arm's-length principle produces structural underfunding of independent organisations (less than 5% of subsidies). The Netherlands' cyclical policy planning and strong private foundation contributions create a multilayered funding ecosystem.

All three countries exceed the European average in internet penetration (DK: 99%, SE: 98.3%, NL: 99%); however, platform preferences and generational patterns require differentiated marketing strategies in each context.

Stakeholder analysis

Expectations, tensions and balance strategies

Funders

Expectations & dynamics

Funders expect measurable outputs: reach, social impact, accountability reports. Civera et al. (2025): relationships are built through stakeholder recognition, continuous dialogue and demonstrable support. Loach et al. (2017): institutions are now evaluated via a triple bottom line: social, economic and environmental impact. The KUBI höringsnotat (2025) reveals that even the funder's own planning process suffers from siloed departmental logic: one department funds a venue, another closes it.

Artists

Roles & needs

Artists are both the most central and most vulnerable stakeholder group. Dansk Kunstnerråd (2025) data: 43% of musicians' artistic income comes from royalties (rettighedsmidler), not institutional fees, reducing institutional dependency but also weakening retention. The institution's core function: enabling aesthetic development without market conformity (Tavano Blessi et al., 2011). Fair Practice Kultur research (Dansk Komponistforening & UKK, 2024): precarity is structural, cross-disciplinary, and requires a collective response, not individual negotiation.

Audiences

Motivation & loyalty patterns

Murray (2005): aesthetic value is secondary; the primary motivation is instrumental social bonding. Copenhagen's Segmenteringsundersøgelsen (Kultur- og Fritidsforvaltningen, 2024) empirically confirms this: social dimension is the top driver; 74% want more leisure time. DELTAG research (308 cultural houses, Nordea-fonden, 2021) maps six participation forms: from attention (passive audience) to ownership (governance participation). Most institutions only cultivate the first. Post-COVID: VibeLab (2025) documents that 'safer space' policies have become mainstream; audiences now expect values-based recognition, not just cultural product.

Volunteers — the fourth stakeholder

A distinct logic, a dual function

Volunteers are not employees, not audience members and not artists. Their motivations, expectations and management needs form a fourth, structurally separate stakeholder layer. In Denmark's kulturhus network alone, 8,900 volunteers contribute alongside 2,600 paid staff (Kulturhusene i Danmark, 2023) — a ratio of 3.4 volunteers per paid employee. They are not supplementing institutional capacity; they are institutional capacity.

The citizenship and integration dimension: Denmark's permanent residence permit system (udlændingeloven § 11) allows applicants to fulfil the aktivt medborgerskab (active citizenship) condition through a minimum of 1 year of documented voluntary work in a qualifying association, directly replacing the medborgerskabsprøve exam (Nyidanmark.dk, 2025). Applicants who meet all four supplementary conditions, including active citizenship, can achieve permanent residence after just 4 years instead of the standard 8. This makes kulturhus volunteering a concrete step on the path to legal security in Denmark. Volunteers in a cultural house thus carry a dual function: they sustain the institution's operational capacity while personally advancing their own integration pathway. This dual function raises an ethical question that aligns with Fair Practice's transparency value: is volunteering truly voluntary when it serves a legal purpose?

What the data shows: VIVE's Frivillighedsundersøgelsen 2024 (Hygum, Fridberg & Bastholm Andrade, 2025) documents that 40% of Danes volunteer, up from 36% in 2020, the first increase in two decades. Average monthly commitment: 18 hours. Average tenure: approximately 13 years. Yet in Copenhagen and major cities, the rate is only 27%, versus 34% in rural areas, meaning kulturhuse operate in the lowest volunteer supply environment in the country. Kulturens Analyseinstitut launched Denmark's first national kulturfrivillighed study in March 2025, underscoring that cultural volunteering has been systematically undermeasured — the measurement paradox operates here too (Kulturens Analyseinstitut, 2025).

Volunteer motivations & tensions

What volunteers need — and what creates friction

Core motivations (research-based): Fællesskab (community belonging) is the strongest and most consistent motivator across all age groups. Meningsfuldhed (meaningfulness) requires feedback not just from the institution but from the people served — Frivillighed.dk (2024) documents that user recognition is as important as organisational recognition. Indflydelse (influence) is critical: MATCH research (Aalborg Universitet & CFSA, 2024) shows volunteers need both clear frameworks and genuine autonomy. For younger volunteers: kompetenceudvikling (skill development) and CV value are explicit motivators they do not see as conflicting with their values.

Generational split: Older volunteers (50+) bring long-term commitment and institutional memory, averaging 13 years. Younger volunteers (18-35) want short-term, project-based, cause-driven engagement — they are not less committed, just differently structured (Kulturens Analyseinstitut, 2024). Only 17% of Danes express no interest in volunteering at all (DIF & DGI, 2023): the supply is there; institutions must meet it on its own terms.

Tensions with other stakeholders: With funders: volunteer hours count as impact evidence, but coordination infrastructure is rarely funded — the measurement paradox has a volunteer dimension. With artists: role boundaries must be explicit to prevent exploitation and identity tension. With audiences: the DELTAG Stage 2→3 pathway — from spectator to volunteer to governance participant — is the institution's most powerful loyalty mechanism and its most underinvested one.

Volunteer engagement strategy

What works — and what doesn't

What doesn't work: Treating volunteering as an informal add-on with no onboarding, no feedback loop, and no progression path. Asking volunteers to fill in for understaffed paid roles without acknowledging the structural imbalance. Counting volunteer hours in annual reports without counting volunteer turnover.

What works: A volunteer coordinator role — even part-time — is the single highest-leverage investment for institutions with 20+ active volunteers. Structured onboarding, a defined role portfolio and annual recognition events reduce turnover dramatically. Most importantly: building an explicit Stage 2→3 pathway means volunteers who become governance participants bring the institution's values into decision-making. This is the Fasching model's deepest lesson — the audience-to-owner pipeline runs through active volunteering.

Core tension: artistic integrity vs. financial sustainability
Fassin (2012): stakeholder management is fundamentally about balancing mutually exclusive demands. Power is asymmetrically distributed: funders with financial resources exercise material power, while artists and local communities depend on management goodwill. BARC/SocialRespons (2024) documented this as the "measurement paradox" in 13 Danish cultural houses: the institution's strongest strategy, relational transformation, is the the least legible to funding logic. Institutions produce impact but cannot prove it; without proof, funding stays constrained.
Findings & analysis

Cross-country synthesis

Across Denmark, Sweden and the Netherlands, the same pattern holds: the funding model determines not only institutional strategy but also stakeholder engagement capacity. No universal framework fits. Each national context produces different requirements for funders, artists, audiences and volunteers.

Finding 1

Institutional sustainability depends on a simultaneous multi-stakeholder approach encompassing funders, artists and audiences (Freeman, 1984). No single stakeholder can be prioritised without structural costs to the others.

Finding 2

The funding system's structure directly shapes marketing strategy. Each national model produces different strategic requirements, confirming that no universal multi-stakeholder engagement framework is adequate for independent cultural institutions.

Finding 3

Digital transformation produces both significant opportunities and structural difficulties (Walmsley, 2016). The primary barrier to digital dialogue is not intent, but structural resource capacity, itself determined by the funding model.

Finding 4: three original contributions to the literature

First: systematic comparative research on independent cultural institutions in a Scandinavian context is extremely limited; this study fills that geographic and methodological gap. Second: existing literature insufficiently addresses the paradoxes in implementing participation and inclusion goals, particularly the relationship between digital communication capacity and funding structure. Third and most original: real-time digital content analysis of six institutions, combined with comprehensive sector reports (VibeLab, 2025; Kultur- og Fritidsforvaltningen, 2024; Dansk Komponistforening & UKK, 2024; Nordea-fonden, 2021), empirically demonstrates that funding model shapes communication strategy, and that no universal model is adequate, substantiating Civera et al.'s (2025) general thesis in a specific sector and geography. Crucially, Walmsley's (2016) digital dialogue potential thesis is both confirmed and bounded: the barrier is not intent, but structural resource capacity determined by the funding model itself.

Institutional comparisons

Denmark: strengths & vulnerabilities
Strengths: Statens Kunstfond's systematic structure; Kulturhusene coordination network; strong digital infrastructure. Vulnerability: High public funding dependency. Tavano Blessi et al. (2011): professional fundraising is not embedded in organisational culture. Bakhshi & Throsby (2010): contraction in both public and private funding is forcing institutions to seek new revenue sources.
Transferable lessons from Sweden & Netherlands
From Sweden: Fasching's musician ownership and audience membership model reduces dependence on state funding while protecting autonomy. From Netherlands: Match-funding, ANBI transparency and Kunstenplan cycle (4-year accountability) offer the most practically transferable mechanisms. Amsterdam's artist-led structures and tax-advantaged foundation model are directly adaptable.

Stakeholder engagement strategy tailored by group

Each stakeholder group requires a different language, channel and approach. The tabs below show what this looks like in practice for funders, artists, audiences and volunteers.

Website transparency section

SROI summary, annual impact report, reach data. FREJ/ANBI foundation status publicly disclosed. Funder accountability demands automatically met; no separate report needed per funder visit.

Sector coordination data

Kulturhusene standard: comparable data from 460 institutions. Not a single institution's voice; the collective sector argument argument. Significantly stronger for policy advocacy.

Numbers + stories together

SROI (Social Return on Investment) measures the social, environmental and economic value an institution creates relative to the resources invested. SROI provides quantitative ground. User stories make relational impact visible. The two work alongside each other, not instead of each other. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

One channel per programme area

Melkweg segmentation: separate Instagram channel per genre/discipline. Content tailored to artist communities: process narrative and development, not event announcements.

R&D platform identity

Workshops, mentoring, residencies at the centre of the website. Artist as "co-creator" not "part of the programme". Preece & Kerrigan (2015): brand value emerges from these interactions.

Visibility builds trust

Artist biography and production process take centre stage. When the institution makes artists visible digitally, the relationship becomes reciprocal, a structural form of fair practice.

Platform by audience age

30+: Facebook community. Young: Instagram discovery. Very young: TikTok reach. Denmark's 99% internet penetration means every audience segment is reachable, but on different platforms.

Two-way dialogue

Active community communication, not passive announcements. Feedback loops, member-only content, post-event conversations. Digital editor: 5% of membership revenue allocated (structural capacity, not good intentions).

Stage 1 → 2 → 3

Observer → Participant → Contributor. Physical space intentionally designed for this transition. Digital content feeds every stage, and the membership model makes Stage 3 governance-level participation possible.

Role portfolio — not just goodwill

Every volunteer needs a written role description: what they do, what decisions they can make independently, and what falls outside their scope. MATCH research (Aalborg Universitet & CFSA, 2024): volunteers want clear frameworks and genuine autonomy simultaneously. Ambiguity is the single biggest driver of early dropout. A role portfolio takes one afternoon to write and changes retention dramatically.

The citizenship dimension

In Denmark, 1 year of documented voluntary work in a qualifying forening fulfils the aktivt medborgerskab condition for permanent residence (udlændingeloven § 11, Nyidanmark.dk, 2025). Applicants meeting all four supplementary conditions qualify after 4 years instead of 8. Kulturhus volunteering is thus a concrete integration pathway. Institutions that acknowledge this openly — and structure it with genuine reciprocity — build the deepest trust. Those that exploit it silently risk the most damaging reputational loss.

Stage 2→3 pipeline

DELTAG (Nordea-fonden, 2021): the transition from volunteer to governance participant is the institution's most powerful loyalty mechanism — and its most underinvested one. Practical structure: volunteer onboarding in month 1, role review in month 6, explicit invitation to board observer role in year 2. Volunteers who see where their engagement leads are far more likely to stay. This is also how the Fasching model works at its deepest level: the audience-to-owner pipeline runs through active volunteering.

Implementation pathway

Four entry points — one direction

The findings of this study point in one direction, but different actors enter from different doors. This pathway is structured around four distinct audiences: boards, funders, volunteers and policy makers. Each has a different lever. None can move the system alone.

For boards

Governance as strategy

The most important governance shift: move from a funder-facing board to a multi-stakeholder board. This means at minimum one artist representative, one audience/member representative, and one volunteer coordinator with board access. This is not tokenism — it is the structural condition for fair decision-making. Fasching's majority artist ownership is the benchmark; even partial representation changes the power dynamic.

Practical steps: revise vedtægter (statutes) to include stakeholder representation clauses. Hold an annual general meeting where membership votes on one board seat. Separate operational decisions (delegated to management) from strategic decisions (retained by board) — this solves the democratic slowness paradox without sacrificing agility. Commission a biennial stakeholder mapping exercise. The board that knows who its stakeholders are makes better decisions than the board that doesn't.

For funders

Rethinking what you're buying

The measurement paradox is partly a funder-created problem. When funders require only quantitative output data — visitor counts, event numbers — institutions produce exactly that and nothing more. The structural fix: add three qualitative indicators to all grant requirements. Return visitor rate (Stage 2 proxy). Volunteer retention rate (community depth proxy). Membership growth (ownership proxy). These cost nothing to collect and change everything about what institutions measure.

For Statens Kunstfond: consider a multi-year driftstilskud track for independent institutions — modelled on Hollandske Kunstenplan's four-year cycle. Short grant cycles force institutions into perpetual application mode, crowding out the relationship-building that creates actual impact. For TrygFonden and Nordea-fonden: fund the measurement infrastructure, not just the activities. An SROI pilot across 10-15 institutions would generate the sector-level evidence base that currently doesn't exist — and make every subsequent grant decision more informed.

For volunteers — and the institutions that work with them

The fourth stakeholder, the overlooked lever

Volunteers carry a dual function in Denmark that makes them strategically unique. Operationally, they sustain institutional capacity: in Denmark's 460 kulturhuse, 8,900 volunteers work alongside 2,600 paid staff (Kulturhusene i Danmark, 2023) — a ratio of 3.4 to 1. Socio-politically, volunteerism in a qualifying forening directly fulfils the aktivt medborgerskab condition for permanent residence under udlændingeloven § 11 (Nyidanmark.dk, 2025). Applicants who meet all four supplementary conditions — including active citizenship — qualify for permanent residence after 4 years instead of 8. A kulturhus volunteer is thus simultaneously sustaining the institution and advancing their own legal pathway in Denmark.

What volunteers need (research-based): VIVE's Frivillighedsundersøgelsen 2024 shows the average tenure is 13 years — but only when the right conditions exist. The MATCH research project (Aalborg Universitet & CFSA, 2024) identifies the core requirements: clear role definition combined with genuine autonomy; recognition from both the institution and the people served (not just one); an explicit progression path from volunteer to governance participant. Only 17% of Danes express no interest in volunteering at all (DIF & DGI, 2023) — the supply is there. The problem is almost always on the institutional side.

The generational dimension: Older volunteers (50+) bring long-term commitment and institutional memory. Younger volunteers (18-35) want short-term, project-based, cause-driven roles — and they see volunteering as legitimate career capital, not a conflict of interest (Kulturens Analyseinstitut, 2024). The institution that designs for both is the institution that builds a sustainable volunteer pipeline. Practical minimum: appoint a volunteer coordinator before reaching 20 active volunteers. Define role portfolios in writing. Build an explicit Stage 2→3 pathway — the DELTAG research (Nordea-fonden, 2021) shows this is the single most underinvested mechanism in Danish cultural houses.

The ethical question Fair Practice demands we ask: When volunteering serves both the institution and the volunteer's legal integration pathway, is it truly voluntary? This is not a reason to avoid the arrangement — it is a reason to be transparent about it. Institutions that acknowledge this dual function honestly, and structure it with clear role boundaries and genuine reciprocity, build the deepest institutional trust. Those that exploit it silently risk the most damaging kind of reputational loss.

For policy makers — municipalities, state, interest organisations

The systemic lever

The single highest-impact policy change available to Copenhagen Municipality: require independent cultural institutions receiving municipal support to be included in Danmarks Statistik's cultural participation data. Currently they are not. Kulturens Selvstændige (2025) documented this explicitly: the sector is statistically invisible, which means it is politically invisible, which means it is chronically underfunded. Visibility in national statistics is not a technical fix — it is a political act.

For Dansk Kunstnerråd: the Mayıs 2026 Fair Practice kodeks vote is the most important single event for the sector this year. Once approved, the next step is lobbying for it to become a subsidy condition — following Hollandske model where all six national funds require compliance. This is a 2027-2028 legislative target. For interest organisations (Kulturhusene i Danmark, Kulturens Selvstændige, Dansk Teater): the KUBI aktör-driven pilot window (2026-2028) is the clearest entry point in a decade. Three to five joint pilot applications — filed collectively, not individually — would demonstrate sector coordination capacity and make the case for a permanent policy framework.

Tensions the model does not resolve

The growth paradox

As membership grows, community feeling risks dilution. Solution: maximum capacity is set , depth, not growth, is the goal (Fasching model).

Democratic governance slowness

Three actor groups = slow decision-making. Solution: operational decisions delegated to management, strategic decisions remain with the board.

Fair Practice cost pressure

Fair artist pay strains the short-term budget. But institutions that do not pay lose good artists , more expensive long-term (Been et al., 2024).

Measurement paradox persists

The strongest impact , trust, belonging, safe space , still cannot be counted. Solution: numbers + stories together, not instead of each other.
